The situation

After Glovo's acquisition by Delivery Hero, I joined as Global UX Director to lead the Barcelona-based product design organization — 65 people at the time, spanning UX designers, researchers, motion designers, UX writers, and localization specialists. The product served users across 25 countries and 19 languages, but the team was operating without a unified research practice or clear design accountability tied to business outcomes.

The mandate was to grow the org, build the research infrastructure, and position design as a driver of Glovo's post-acquisition growth story — not just a production service.

What we built

We grew the Barcelona team from 65 to 105, hiring across all five disciplines while restructuring around Glovo's key revenue areas. Rather than a centralized service model, I built a federated structure where designers were embedded in product squads and held accountable for business metrics, not just delivery.

I built the company's first UX research practice from scratch — introducing dogfooding rituals to identify competitive gaps firsthand and establishing quarterly research cycles that fed directly into roadmap decisions. I led two company-wide hackathons with the CPO and CEO, rapidly prototyping consumer features: homepage redesign, social shopping, shared payment flows, loyalty program redesign, and catalogue discovery.

The team delivered CRM and promotions personalization across the consumer app — a multi-month initiative coordinated across product, engineering, and data science — resulting in a 15% lift in new customer acquisition and 20% improvement in retention with no degradation to LTV.

I led a company-wide font replacement and localization overhaul, replacing licensed fonts with an open alternative across all 19 languages on a 24-hour turnaround SLA. That single initiative saved €500k annually in licensing costs. I simultaneously renegotiated the design tooling stack, cutting a further €500k in annual software spend.

Left: the team on a research and inspiration trip to San Francisco. Right: a subset of the team at Glovo's Barcelona HQ.

What I learned

Leading a team through a post-acquisition period means managing two things at once: the external work of shipping product that moves the business, and the internal work of rebuilding cohesion and shared purpose. Teams that don't trust the direction ship slowly, and teams that don't ship lose trust in the direction.

Growing from 65 to 105 made clear that hiring quality has a direct multiplier effect on everything else. Every person we brought in raised or lowered the bar for the whole org — and that discipline in hiring is something I've carried into every role since.
