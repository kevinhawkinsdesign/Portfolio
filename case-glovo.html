The situation

After Glovo's acquisition by Delivery Hero — combining operations that together served a population of 2.2 billion people — I joined as Global Director of Design to lead the Barcelona-based organization. The team covered five disciplines: UX design, research, motion design, UX writing, and localization. The product served 25 countries and 19 languages, but the org was operating without a unified research practice or clear design accountability tied to business outcomes.

The mandate was to grow the team, build the research infrastructure, and position design as a driver of Glovo's post-acquisition growth — not just a production service to product squads.

Building the org

We grew the Barcelona team from 65 to 105, hiring across all five disciplines while restructuring around Glovo's key revenue areas. Rather than a centralized service model, I built a federated structure where designers were embedded in product squads and held accountable for business metrics, not just delivery velocity.

I built the company's first UX research practice from scratch — introducing competitive dogfooding rituals and establishing quarterly research cycles that fed directly into roadmap decisions. A defining output was a comprehensive competitive analysis of delivery apps across markets: examining how Talabat, FoodPanda, and PedidosYa handled onboarding, category navigation, and search — and turning those findings into actionable redesign priorities for our own consumer app.

Consumer app redesign and brand partnerships

I led two CEO/CPO hackathons, rapidly prototyping new consumer features: homepage redesign, social shopping, shared payment flows, loyalty program redesign, and catalogue discovery. The redesigned consumer app introduced personalized picks, social proof signals, and a refined category and restaurant discovery experience.

One of the highest-profile deliverables during this period was the Disney·Pixar brand integration — an Inside Out 2 takeover of the Glovo app, bringing animated characters into the product experience while maintaining usability and conversion. It required close creative and technical collaboration across brand, product, and engineering, and demonstrated what was possible when design had a seat in commercial partnership conversations.

What I learned

Leading a team through a post-acquisition period means managing two things simultaneously: the external work of shipping product that moves the business, and the internal work of rebuilding cohesion and shared purpose after ownership changes. Teams that don't trust the direction ship slowly, and teams that don't ship lose trust in the direction.

The font replacement project taught me something I didn't expect: the highest-leverage design decisions are often the least glamorous. A typeface change across 19 languages saved €500k and improved brand consistency globally. That kind of systems thinking — understanding where design touches operations and cost — is what earns design a seat in business conversations, not just product reviews.
