BookClub  ·  Director of Product Design  ·  2020–2022

Building a 0→1 product design function
for a social reading platform

Joined as founding designer and grew the function to Director level — defining the product vision, research practice, and design system that connected readers directly with the authors they love.

The situation

BookClub was a startup with a novel premise: giving readers direct access to the authors of the books they love, through author-led book clubs, live Q&As, and community discussion. The model was differentiated — but the product, research practice, and design infrastructure needed to be built from scratch.

I joined as the founding designer. There was no design system, no research process, no team structure, and no documentation. My job was to build all of it while also shipping the product features that would determine whether the business worked.

What we built

I started where every founding designer should: with the users. I ran the first structured research program at BookClub, conducting interviews with both readers and authors to understand what made the author relationship feel meaningful — and what broke it. Those early findings shaped the product architecture we built everything else on top of.

The core product challenge was a two-sided marketplace problem: readers needed enough authors to find someone they loved, and authors needed enough engaged readers to make their time feel worthwhile. I designed the author onboarding experience and club creation flow specifically to reduce time-to-value for authors — so their first interaction with the platform felt like a real community, not an empty room.

I designed and documented the first design system — giving the growing engineering team a shared component library and giving any future designers a foundation to build on. I also established the first UX research practice, introducing regular user interviews, usability testing, and a feedback loop with the product team that turned qualitative insight into sprint priorities.

As the product matured, I grew into a Director role — defining the roadmap vision with the CPO, managing contractor designers, and representing the user in leadership conversations. That transition from solo practitioner to team leader within a single role gave me a clarity about the difference between doing design and enabling it that I've carried through every leadership role since.

What I learned

Building 0→1 teaches you to distinguish between what users say they want and what actually keeps them coming back. At BookClub, readers said they wanted more authors. What they actually needed was a better experience in the clubs they were already in — stronger community features, easier discussion tools, and a sense of progress through the reading journey. The data told that story; the interviews pointed us toward it.

It also taught me the value of documentation as leverage. Every research finding I wrote down, every design decision I annotated, every component I specified — those became the institutional knowledge that let the team move faster as we scaled. The investment in documentation is always worth it, even when the timeline pressure suggests otherwise.
