The situation

Booking.com operates within the Booking Holdings portfolio — a network that includes Kayak, Priceline, Agoda, and Rentalcars. In practice, that means product decisions don't end at Booking.com's borders. Features that touch the core travel experience require alignment across brands, platforms, and engineering teams with very different timelines and priorities.

As UX Team Lead, I led two product teams working at that intersection — responsible not just for the design quality within Booking.com, but for getting cross-brand concepts from idea through alignment to production.

What we built

I led two internal hackathon projects that went all the way through to live production — a rare outcome in an organization of Booking.com's scale. The first was a group travel planning feature, allowing multiple travelers to coordinate itineraries and book collaboratively. The second was a local events and activities integration, surfacing curated experiences directly within the accommodation booking flow.

Both features required not just strong design execution, but the organizational work of getting engineering, product, and legal teams across Booking Holdings brands aligned around a shared roadmap. I owned the cross-functional facilitation directly.

On the operations side, I led the development of the Connected Stay roadmap — a multi-quarter planning artifact that mapped the complete guest experience from booking through check-out, coordinating contributions from product, tech, and commercial teams across the business.

What I learned

Getting a prototype to production inside a large tech company is almost entirely an organizational problem — not a design one. The design work is the easy part. The hard part is building enough trust across enough stakeholders that they're willing to reorganize engineering resources, align on scope, and accept the risk of something new. I learned to treat that stakeholder work as design work — to prototype it, test it, and iterate on it the same way I would a user flow.
