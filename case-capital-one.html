Two Tracks

Research that grounded a platform I helped design and ship.

The 2016–2017 engagement split into two connected workstreams: deep-dive research into how small businesses actually handle travel and expense, and the information architecture, design system, and interface work for ICCE Web, Capital One's unified virtual payments platform. Research

The Ecosystem Concept Model

A cross-functional team spanning product management, design, research, content strategy, and analytics split into two workstreams, Mobile T&E and Web P-Card, and ran a holistic research effort from the ground up: planning and recruiting, scripted interviews, grassroots exercises with Capital One associates, journey mapping, competitive analysis against tools like Expensify, Concur, and eBillity, and solution ideation sessions.

For the ICCE (Integrated Commercial Card Experience) initiative, I led 20+ in-person empathy interviews with small business owners, phone interviews with existing customers for the Virtual Card program, and 6 taped webcam sessions for remote validation, across multiple full-day sessions and geographies.

User archetypes translated into a features roadmap

Recurring themes cut across almost every interview: people building their own workarounds because the official process couldn't keep up, heavy manual data entry, rules communicated ad hoc rather than made clear upfront, and a surprising amount of emotional weight tied to simply being trusted with a corporate card. One line from an interview stuck with the whole team:

I didn't get into this job to submit expense reports.

From this research I built a comprehensive Ecosystem Concept Model: mapping all the tasks a small business owner performs at every cadence (daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly, annually) against their internal attitudes and external relationships. This radial model became the foundation for SMB product strategy and was adopted by multiple product teams.

Key insights surfaced three user archetypes: ICCE Mobile (T&E), Virtual Card Users, and Decision Makers, each translated into driving principles and a features roadmap that moved insights directly into planning. The personas behind them spanned a wide trust spectrum: a Director of IT who ties his sense of being respected to doing his expense report right every time, and a tile installer with no corporate card at all, fronting her own money and losing hours a month tracking receipts in a folder in her truck. Serving that whole range, not just the average case, is what the roadmap had to do: OCR (optical character recognition) to auto-capture receipt data, and auto-generated expense reports tied to the billing cycle instead of a monthly scramble.

That direction shipped as Capital One T&Easy, the corporate cardholder app: balance and spend limit visibility, the ability to lock or unlock a card on the spot, transaction notifications, and expense capture on the go, the mobile-first answer to almost everything the research surfaced.

The Business Case

Unifying four disconnected tools into one platform

Before ICCE, Capital One's commercial card customers had to stitch together four separate platforms just to manage their account, rewards, expenses, and virtual card program. EAP (electronic accounts payable) was the fastest-growing sub-product in the commercial card industry at the time, forecast at roughly 17% CAGR, and the fragmentation was actively costing ground against dedicated competitors like Verient.

ICCE Web was the fix: one integrated commercial card experience serving five distinct customer segments, from Basic self-service accounts to Large Corporate clients running full T&E and EAP programs, across roughly 3,700 clients. The rollout was phased across four releases, starting with a mobile pilot and building to full platform rollout, under a 5-year B2B payables strategy written by Fouad Alkisswani, then Director and Head of Commercial B2B Product Strategy & Digital Channels, who secured $100M+ in CEO-level investment for it. The platform this work fed into went on to post 30% CAGR revenue growth over the following two years.

Information Architecture & Design System

Three sides of one platform

ICCE Web had to work for three different audiences at once: the buying company (basic users, program admins, and Capital One's own issuer-side view), the vendors receiving virtual card payments, and Capital One's internal servicing teams. I mapped the full site structure across all three before any screen got designed, so navigation, permissions, and content ownership were settled up front instead of getting renegotiated screen by screen.

Alongside the IA, I contributed to the ICCE Web style guide: an atomic system of color, typography, icons, and components (forms, alerts, modals, data visualization) built so the product could scale consistently across every release. The team ran on an agile PI (Program Increment) and sprint cadence, with a formal design QA pass at the end of each cycle, one pass alone tracked 75 polish items across the Add Vendor, Pay Vendor, and Dashboard flows.

Shipping Payables

From annotated wireframe to shipped screen

The Payables flow, where a buying company tracks, filters, and pays vendor invoices via virtual card, went through detailed annotated wireframes before visual design: filter relationships, search field weighting, virtual card issuance and cancellation, and every payment status from Pending through Accepted, Declined, and Expired.

That specification work fed directly into the final product: a dashboard giving buying companies a single view of their virtual payments (processed, open, credited, expired), net paid over time, and outstanding tasks like expiring cards or declined vendor payments.

User Management

Giving admins control over who has access

Program admins needed to manage who on their team could see and act on payments, so I designed the full user management flow: a searchable directory, adding and onboarding new users, editing roles and permissions, and unlocking, deactivating, and reactivating accounts, each with its own confirmation state and one-time-PIN verification for sensitive changes.

Project Elements

The credit card, radically redesigned

Project Elements by Capital One Labs was the most ambitious work I contributed to during this engagement. The premise: the credit card is a physical object you carry every day. It should feel like it belongs to you, not your bank.

The credit card is a physical object you carry every day. It should feel like it belongs to you, not your bank.

The project reimagined every aspect of the card: its shape, material (bamboo, copper, hammered metal, and other finishes across 12 materials and finishes total), and visual identity, free of traditional card branding. Cards had names: Bamboo. Coral. Lemon. Each told a story about the person who carried it.

The project launched as the Capital One House, a physical installation at Antone's in Austin during SXSW 2017, a modular, gallery-style space where roughly 12,000 attendees could interact with card prototypes, explore materials, and experience a version of financial product design that treated the cardholder as a creative collaborator.

Capital One Café

Banking meets hospitality

I contributed UX strategy to the Capital One Café initiative, the bank's flagship physical-meets-digital experience designed to make financial services approachable and community-oriented. I helped design the welcoming kiosk experience and ambient digital displays, reinforcing the "everyone's welcome" positioning at every touchpoint.

Legacy

The same divide the research surfaced, still live today

Years later, Capital One's commercial and business card experience still splits along close to the same line our research mapped: dedicated portals like Commercial Card Connect and Intellix give corporate admins the spend controls, virtual card issuance, and AP oversight our Decision Maker and Web personas needed, while the standard mobile app carries the lighter, on-the-go card management our Mobile T&E personas needed. Different products now, but the same underlying divide we designed around then.

That same design rigor carried into other tracks running alongside mine. Lauren Howell, a design peer from the same era, later patented a visual UX system for Capital One Mobile Wallet that reimagined card access and onboarding for 1M+ users.

It shows up in how people talk about Capital One's apps today, too: a usability teardown of the Capital One Mobile app and a recurring sentiment on Reddit land on the same praise: "capital one's entire online system is heads above any of the other multiple institutions I bank with... everything is set up clearly, intuitively", one Redditor's summary of the consumer app, a different product than the commercial platform I worked on, but built inside the same design organization and culture.

Reflection

On designing for the full spectrum

The widest range in this project wasn't the number of platforms, it was the users. A Director of IT protecting his reputation with a spotless expense report and a tile installer fronting her own money because she has no corporate card at all are both "T&E users," but they needed almost nothing in common from the product. Good enterprise research means resisting the urge to design for the average of that range, because the average user doesn't exist.
