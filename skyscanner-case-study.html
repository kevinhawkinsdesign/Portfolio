Infrastructure scales. Headcount doesn't. And in an AI-native world, the infrastructure question becomes: what does democratised design capability look like when AI is the enabling layer?

02
02 · Vision

Don't hire more designers.
Make everyone a better designer.

The world was already moving toward PMs designing, engineers designing, and agents designing. The question wasn't how to protect design. It was how to raise the floor for everyone doing design work, while freeing senior designers for the work only they could do.

This became a technical architecture: one governed AI backbone, a growing library of shared capabilities, a pipeline that removed translation between design and engineering, and a monitoring system that caught quality drift before it became a problem.

"Your goal: make every PM able to come to a design crit and not get laughed out of the room. What do we need to make that happen?"
Oli Eliel · Director of Design, Skyscanner
The strategic bet

AI fluency is a design infrastructure problem

Most companies treated AI adoption as individual responsibility. We treated it as an organisational infrastructure problem. That framing changed everything: there was a system to build, not just a behaviour to encourage.

Three-layer model

Capability · Governance · Culture

Every infrastructure decision operated across three layers. Build the capability. Govern it. Change the culture. Miss any layer and the others fail.

The outcome goal: 30% of product work fully automated. 60% partially automated. Only the most complex 10% requiring full human planning end-to-end.

04 · Culture Change: The Resistance

The resistance came from
exactly where you wouldn't expect

Convention says seniors resist AI and juniors embrace it. At Monta it was the opposite, and understanding why changed the entire education approach.

Resistance 1: Less experienced roles

Analysis paralysis: too much unknown

Designers and researchers asked to play new roles froze, not from the tools, but from uncertainty. They couldn't detect AI slop. They couldn't tell good output from bad. Terminal basics, prompt engineering, LLM fundamentals: the mountain felt too high. They froze.

Can't detect slopAnalysis paralysisUnknown unknowns
Why seniors didn't resist

Pattern recognition made them dangerous, in the best way

Principal and staff-level designers and engineers were already incentivised to do more with less. They had experience to detect slop, catch hallucinations, fix prompt engineering. The tools amplified skills they already had. They dove in immediately.

Resistance 2: Engineers

PR overload and quality debt, not territory

Engineers weren't territorial. They were pragmatic. The designer-to-code pipeline skyrocketed PRs. Buggy code was being produced faster, paradoxically slowing engineers down, as they were on the hook for QA, code review, and merge conflict resolution regardless of who wrote it.

PR volume explosionQuality debtReview overhead

Resolution: PR quality gates, skill-reviewed code templates, and an explicit shared definition of "merge-ready," separating AI generation from quality validation.

The honest trade-off

"85 things shipped today, and I had no idea about any of them."

Speed was real. So was the governance challenge. The pipeline scaled velocity faster than oversight could follow. Buggy code produced faster than engineers could catch it. PRs overflowing. This created the very problem that led to the monitoring layer, and the sequencing was a mistake. Governance should have shipped with the pipeline.

Quality gates before the queue

Shared standards for "merge-ready". Skill-reviewed templates. Separated generation step (fast, exploratory) from quality step (reviewed, tested). Engineers reviewed validated output, not raw AI generation.

06 · System 4: AI Usage Monitoring

You can't govern
what you can't see

When AI runs at scale across every team, process consistency becomes harder than capability, and requires its own infrastructure

The problem

Wildly divergent processes, all producing different quality

The infrastructure worked. Team workflows diverged. Some used AI brilliantly. Others wasted tokens on duplicated prompts: 100 people writing 100 slightly different versions of the same task. I had no visibility without a system.

What I built

Token usage dashboard: role, region, seniority

Deeper telemetry than a standard deployment. I could surface: token volume per person/role/region/seniority; most common request types clustered by team; similar-but-different prompts to unify as shared skills; seniority-level patterns; teams diverging from standards before it became a quality problem.

The weekly digest loop

Top 50 patterns → cherry-pick → action

Every week: top 50 usage patterns across all teams. Design Director and I reviewed together. Cherry-picked: which patterns needed governance? Which were ready to promote to shared skills? Which teams drifting? A signal, not a gate. A digest, not a bottleneck.

Next step (in progress at departure)

Global skills for the top 50 most common use cases

Rather than 100 people writing 100 different prompts for the same task, codifying the best version of each into a shared skill. Reduces token waste. Standardises quality. A reference point, not a restriction.

Reduce wasteStandardise qualityEnable without gatekeeping

This monitoring layer should have shipped with the pipeline, not after. Capability and governance always ship together. That's the lesson I carry forward.

07 · The 30-Agent ADLC System

An end-to-end product development
automation engine

Inspired by Booking.com's experimentation culture, built for an AI-native context. Signals from every source, automated from detection to verification.

Fully automated

30%

Entire flow from signal to shipped: no human required. AI detects, classifies, scopes, designs, builds, and verifies independently.

Partially automated

60%

AI handles detection, classification, and sizing. Humans strategise and approve. AI then builds and verifies.

Fully human

10%

The most complex, ambiguous, or strategically sensitive work: full human planning, research, and execution end-to-end.

Signal sources: social media comments, App Store reviews (all markets, all languages), SDR/AE/CSM touchpoints, public roadmap submissions (roadmap.monta.com), PostHog analytics, and support ticket clusters. Everything connected. Nothing manually entered.

What Sense does

Detects signals the human eye would miss

Agents monitored every source continuously, including microscopic app store reviews in languages never surfacing in English dashboards. Portuguese 1-star reviews. Korean driver frustrations from a specific city. Patterns too small to bubble up manually, but real enough to indicate systemic issues. Nothing manually entered.

🗂️
Stage 2
CLASSIFY
Fibonacci scoringComplexity assessmentTheme bundlingPopularity weightingAuto vs. human routingRoadmap status update
What Classify does

Turns raw signals into prioritised, sized, routed problems

Applied Fibonacci scoring to every signal, determined complexity and scope, bundled similar signals into thematic problem statements weighted by popularity: how many independent sources flagged the same issue?

Critical: Classify made the automation/human routing decision. Could this be solved by an agent? Or did a human need to strategise? Public roadmap submissions were automatically ingested, classified, and used to update public status, closing the loop with customers without a human touching a single item.
